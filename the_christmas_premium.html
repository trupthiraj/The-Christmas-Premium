The Christmas Premium¶

What London's Airbnb data reveals about the world's most¶

famous festive city and exactly how much the magic costs

London at Christmas is not a marketing campaign. It is a cultural export. The lights on Oxford Street. The markets in Hyde Park and South Bank. The ice rinks outside Somerset House and the Natural History Museum. Every year, millions of people from every corner of the world put London in December on their list and the city's short term rental market prices accordingly.

This project uses Inside Airbnb listing data across London's neighbourhoods alongside Google Trends search interest for "London Christmas" to answer the question every traveller has asked and every hospitality brand wants to know: how much does the Christmas premium actually cost, where does it land hardest, and which neighbourhoods are quietly extracting the most value from the world's collective festive imagination?

The data does not care about the fairy lights. But it noticed them.

Dataset 1: Inside Airbnb London Listings, September 2025 Dataset 2: Google Trends, "London Christmas" 2020-2025 Tools: Python · pandas · Plotly · Tableau Public Analyst: Trupthi Raj

# === DATA CLEANING ===

# Step 1 — Remove missing prices
df_listings = df_listings[df_listings['price'].notna()].copy()
print(f"After removing missing prices: {len(df_listings):,} rows")

# Step 2 — Remove price outliers
# Keep listings between £10 and £5,000 per night
df_listings = df_listings[
    (df_listings['price'] >= 10) &
    (df_listings['price'] <= 5000)
].copy()
print(f"After removing price outliers: {len(df_listings):,} rows")

# Step 3 — Remove extreme minimum nights
# Keep listings with minimum nights of 30 or less
df_listings = df_listings[
    df_listings['minimum_nights'] <= 30
].copy()
print(f"After removing extreme minimum nights: {len(df_listings):,} rows")

# Step 4 — Keep only Entire home and Private room
# Drop Shared room and Hotel room — too few and not relevant
df_listings = df_listings[
    df_listings['room_type'].isin(['Entire home/apt', 'Private room'])
].copy()
print(f"After keeping relevant room types: {len(df_listings):,} rows")

# Step 5 — Add price bands
def price_band(price):
    if price < 100:
        return 'Budget (under £100)'
    elif price < 200:
        return 'Mid-range (£100-£200)'
    elif price < 350:
        return 'Premium (£200-£350)'
    else:
        return 'Luxury (£350+)'

df_listings['price_band'] = df_listings['price'].apply(price_band)

# Step 6 — Add availability category
def availability_category(days):
    if days < 60:
        return 'Low availability'
    elif days < 180:
        return 'Moderate availability'
    else:
        return 'High availability'

df_listings['availability_category'] = df_listings[
    'availability_365'].apply(availability_category)

# Step 7 — Reset index
df_listings.reset_index(drop=True, inplace=True)

# Step 8 — Add Christmas season flag to Google Trends
df_trends['season'] = df_trends['Month'].dt.month.map({

The London Airbnb Landscape¶

Before the Christmas premium can be measured, the baseline needs to be understood. London's short term rental market is not one market. It is thirty three micro-markets wearing the same postcode system, each with its own pricing logic, its own demand profile, and its own relationship with the kind of traveller who chooses Airbnb over a hotel.

Westminster has 11,385 listings and an average nightly price of £309. Barking and Dagenham has 200 listings and an average price that tells a completely different story about who travels where and why. The geography of London's Airbnb market is the geography of London itself — central is expensive, outer is affordable, and the zones in between are where the most interesting pricing stories live.

This first analysis maps the full landscape. Every borough. Every price point. The foundation that every subsequent finding builds on.

The Room Type Premium¶

Not all listings in the same borough are created equal. A private room in Westminster and an entire apartment in Westminster are two completely different products at two completely different price points targeting two completely different travellers. The room type split matters because it tells you something about who each borough is actually serving and at what price point the Christmas visitor is making their decision.

London's Airbnb market splits roughly 68% entire homes to 32% private rooms after cleaning. But that split is not uniform across boroughs. Inner London skews heavily toward entire homes because the traveller profile is wealthier and the minimum stay requirements are longer. Outer London has a higher proportion of private rooms because the host profile is more casual and the price sensitivity of the guest is higher.

The chart below shows average pricing by room type across every borough. The gap between entire home and private room pricing in each borough is the measure of how polarised each local market actually is.

The Christmas Search Signal¶

The world starts thinking about Christmas in London in August. Not December. Not even November. August. When the average person is sitting on a beach somewhere, half their brain is already calculating whether this is the year they finally do the Winter Wonderland, the Somerset House ice rink, the Kew Gardens lights. The Google Trends data caught it.

Search interest for "London Christmas" sits at 6.3 on average in August. By September it is 11.8. October brings it to 19.0. November nearly triples it to 42.8. December peaks at 60.0 on average across the five years in this dataset, with December 2025 hitting the maximum score of 100.

This is not a spike. It is a build. A four month acceleration that the short term rental market prices into every listing in every borough whether the host consciously thinks about it or not. The search interest is the demand signal. The pricing data is the supply response. The chart below shows exactly when the world decides it wants to be in London for Christmas and how consistently that decision has been made year after year.

The Scarcity Premium¶

Price tells you what a host thinks their listing is worth. Availability tells you whether the market agrees. A listing priced at £400 per night with 300 days of availability is a listing the market has not validated. A listing priced at £400 with 30 days of availability is a listing that sells itself.

The relationship between price and availability is where the Christmas premium becomes most visible. The boroughs that combine high pricing with low availability are the ones where demand structurally exceeds supply. These are not just expensive boroughs. They are boroughs where the expensive listings are actually being booked.

For a Christmas traveller trying to find somewhere to stay in London in December, this chart is the most useful thing in this analysis. It shows not just where is expensive but where is expensive and full. Those are two very different problems requiring two very different solutions.

The Solo Host vs The Empire Builder¶

Not everyone on Airbnb is renting out their spare room while they visit family for the weekend. London's short term rental market has two very different host profiles operating in the same space with very different pricing strategies and very different relationships with demand.

The solo host has one listing. It is probably their home or a property they own and manage personally. The pricing reflects what they think their space is worth, the availability reflects their actual life, and the reviews reflect a personal investment in the guest experience that a professional operator cannot always replicate at scale.

The empire builder has ten, twenty, sometimes fifty listings. This is a business. The pricing is optimised, the availability is managed, and the operation runs with the efficiency of someone who has done this enough times to know exactly what the market will bear. Whether they charge more or less than the solo host, and whether their listings are more or less available, tells you something important about how professionalised London's short term rental market has become.

Empire builders charge £264 on average. Solo hosts charge £161. The assumption that personal, character-filled listings command a premium over professionally managed ones is not what the London Airbnb data says. The professional operator has figured out the pricing optimisation. The solo host has not, or has not tried to.

But the availability chart tells the other half of the story. Solo hosts average 200 available days per year, the lowest of any host category. Empire builders average 222 days. The solo host listing is harder to book not because the host is more selective but because the demand is absorbing the supply faster. People are choosing the personal listing at £161 and booking it out. People are choosing the empire builder listing at £264 and finding it still available.

The Christmas traveller looking for somewhere to stay in London in December faces this choice in every borough. The characterful flat managed by someone who lives nearby and leaves a bottle of wine on the counter. Or the professionally managed apartment with the keypad entry and the laminated instruction sheet. The data says the first one costs less and is harder to find. The second one costs more and is usually still there when you look.

So. What Does Christmas Actually Cost In London?¶

It depends entirely on where you stay, who you book with, and how far in advance you start looking. Which the data suggests should be August. Not December. August.

Westminster charges £309 on average. Croydon charges £96. These are not two different cities. They are two stops on the same tube line and the price difference between them is the price of proximity to the fairy lights, the markets, the ice rinks, and the particular kind of London that the world flies in to experience every December. The Christmas premium is not a myth. It is a postcode.

The search interest data makes one thing very clear. Demand for London at Christmas has grown every single year in this dataset. 2025 hit a Google Trends score of 100, the highest ever recorded for this search term. The world is not getting less interested in spending Christmas in London. It is getting more interested, earlier, with more intention. The hosts know this. The pricing reflects it. The availability data confirms that the best listings in the best boroughs absorb that demand faster than any other category in this dataset.

The empire builder charges more. The solo host sells out faster. The premium borough is expensive and relatively scarce. The affordable borough is cheap and always available. None of this is surprising in isolation. What the data makes visible is the full shape of the market at once, every borough, every host type, every price point, laid out clearly enough that the Christmas traveller and the hospitality brand investing in London short term rentals can make a genuinely informed decision.

The fairy lights are not in the data. But everything else is.
